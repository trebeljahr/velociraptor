About Raptor Runner

A pixel-art homage to the Chrome "No Internet" dinosaur game — with a running velociraptor, a full day/night cycle, and a starry sky.

The story

I made the first version of this game a few years ago, mostly for fun. It already had the thing that matters: a core loop that felt good to play, a charming pixel-art raptor, a blue sky, a handful of cacti. For a quick weekend thing, it worked. I shelved it anyway — the kind of shelving where you tell yourself you'll come back to it and then years quietly go past.

What you're playing now is that same game, brought back and polished with the time and care I never found the first time around. The bones are still the same. Most of the new work is the finishing — the thousand small things between "runs" and "feels finished".

What "polishing" means, in practice

If you've only ever shipped the fun 80% of a side project, it's easy to underestimate the last 20%. Polish isn't glamorous — it's a pile of quiet decisions, most of which nobody will consciously notice, but which together make the difference between "cute" and "finished". Some of what went into this one:

  • Tuning jump physics until they feel deliberate instead of floaty — and making the whole loop frame-rate independent, so the game feels the same on a 60Hz laptop, a 120Hz phone, and a throttled background tab.
  • Drawing hand-authored, concave collision polygons for every cactus so near-misses actually read as near-misses instead of punishing the player.
  • Building a full day/night cycle where the sky color, the sun and moon, and the lighting on the raptor, cacti, and ground are all one coherent system — so dusk genuinely reads as dusk on the whole scene, not just in the sky.
  • A rotating night sky with a Milky Way band, stars that twinkle at different rates, and the occasional shooting star on longer runs. None of it matters individually. Together, the sky feels alive.
  • Measuring the exact head position in every frame of the run animation, so head-mounted cosmetics sit on the raptor instead of bobbing around above it.
  • A proper start screen, a settings menu, music and SFX with a remembered mute preference, persistent personal bests, unlockable cosmetics, a shareable score card rendered in a web worker, an offline install path, real accessibility labels, and all the other small quality-of-life things a finished web game is supposed to have.

None of this is hard in isolation. All of it, together, is the part I never had the patience for before.

Why now

Honestly? Because AI pair programming finally made the polishing fun. The grunt work that used to feel like a chore — chasing a floaty frame, reworking a compositing pass, writing copy for the fifth time — is now a conversation, and that conversation has a collaborator who doesn't get bored. I have a pile of half-finished side projects I intend to walk back through with the same energy.

Raptor Runner is the first of them. If you enjoyed it, stick around — there are more coming.
